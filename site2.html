RESEARCH

My research focuses on 3D printed, functional, biomimetic materials, structures, and mechanisms. I am also interested in parametric 4D printing and compostable biocomposite filaments, and their potential applications. Furthermore, an interdisciplinary and sustainable approach is chosen for my research.

Hygrobot: Designing and manufacturing of a 3D printed, moisture-driven and sustainable soft robot

Currently, environmental monitoring is time consuming, requires training, produces pollution and, due to human interference, distorted results. This is why autonomous, compostable, and self-locomotive sensing platforms are of great interest. In this interdisciplinary project, a complete product development process was carried out; the result is a functioning, energy-autonomous, biodegradable, and mesoscaled soft robot, which could be used to collect data and explore an environment. Other applications include pollution detection (heavy metals), alerts for mitigation of natural disasters (wildfires) and more comprehensive modelling of climate change effects on ecosystems. In near future, even aerial seeding, space exploration (Terraforming), and hybrid electromechanical systems could be conceivable. There is still untapped potential for hygromorphic soft robots, since almost no research has been conducted on them.

The final Hygrobot successfully used hygroscopic, choreographed, and sequential movements (velocity: 2-3 cm/day). It could be shown that knowledge from beam theory is applicable for Hygrobots’ performance evaluation. The design was based on an evolutionary algorithm, which unveiled novel locomotion tactics (crawling, walking, rolling). The examined filament (Laywood Meta5) consisted of 20-40% wood (beech fibers). In wet state, its elasticity increased 10-fold and its weight by 75%. Due to the quick and high water absorption capacity, the height of a cuboid made out of this material can increase by 80%.

The video on the right shows how a hygromorphic bilayer reacts to humidity fluctuations. Hygrobots utilise such moisture-dependent deformations as a propulsion mechanism.

This work achieved advances on different levels: It summarizes existent papers and explains emergent scientific fields. Besides that, it introduced an entire manufacturing workflow to create Hygrobots. This work is far from finished. Future goals include: Showcasing real applications, automatization/ formalization/ democratization of the design process, achieve robust/ reliable/ fast locomotion, discover novel materials and the development of functional and compostable biosensors, which would allow the Hygrobots to sense their environment.

On the verge of engineering and nature...

I love observing mechanisms and structures from nature and recreating them artificially. In the slideshow are pictures of some of my recent biological inspirations.

Image descriptions: (1) Pine cones with atypical scales that open horizontally. (2) Common but very large cone of a Canary Island pine. (3) Cones with unusual morphological features. The hooks are used e.g. for zoochory (animal-dependent dispersal). (4/5) Seeds with different tactics for air-dependent dispersal (anemochory). (6) Gall midge larva with the ability for ballistic, hydraulic catapulting. (7) Burdock plant that served as inspiration for Velcro. Also shown are its small hooks that interlock with tiny loops. (8) Images of the pollination mechanism of a meadow sage flower triggered by a pencil . (9) Plant species with water-reppellent and self-cleaning properties (similar to Lotus/Salvinia effect). (10) Dried poppy capsule and its internal structures. Close-up of the countless seeds that fall sideways out of the radially symmetrical openings during wind-induced vibrations.
